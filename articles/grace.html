(css: "font-size: 150%;font-family: 'stenciletta-solid';")[What’s Flash?] Flash is a software platform for creating and displaying multimedia content. It comes in two parts, a tool to make animated content (this has been rebranded as Adobe Animate, it won’t be affected by the discontinuation) , and a downloadable browser plugin called Flash Player whose discontinuation is what we’re going to talk about. By downloading Flash Player onto your web browser, you can then play and display Flash content on the internet. By 2009 99% of computer users connected to the internet had it (and Flash Player was even available on the Nintendo Wii!). (css: "font-size: 150%;font-family: 'stenciletta-solid';")[A Brief History of Flash] • It started life in 1993 as a vector-based web animation tool called FutureSplash, made by FutureWave Software. • It was bought by Macromedia in 1996, who rebranded it as Flash and distributed the free Flash Player plugin for browsers. • By 2005, Flash Player had been installed in more computers than any other media player • It was acquired that same year by Adobe Systems, who’ve held onto it ever since. • Adobe announced in 2017 that it would end support for Flash on December 31st 2020. Digital data can be har to preserve because it needs to be mediated by something, technology, software or otherwise, unlike an old document or artifact that you can look at to get a sense of what it was. The preservation of digital media can require more intentionality, to keep it compatible with current technology, or to have older technology on hand, which in itself needs maintenance. Digital archivists face some unique technical and legal challenges to their work. Sometimes software is missing source code, and without it, can’t be adapted for modern technology. Sometimes archivists also need to bypass copyright protection for abandonware (software whose rights-owners were uncontactable, but whose copyright still stands) which puts the [[legality of the archival work|5.1]] into question. And while digital storage can hold and incredible amount of data, at a fraction of the space of a traditional archive, there’s conversely an even more incredible amount of data online, making it difficult find and assess what’s worth keeping. Flashpoint has had to work around many of these issues, whether through clever programming to get around sitelocked games, carefully curating and organising their collected games or negotiating with companies and creators that still retain ownership of their games.
